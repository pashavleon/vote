Africa’s referee of the year barred from the World Cup at Miami airport

Updated 11 June 2026 · TOP FAN VOTE editorial · Draft

Omar Abdulkadir Artan was on FIFA’s list of 52 World Cup referees and was set to become the first Somali official at a World Cup finals. Instead, he was denied entry to the United States at Miami International Airport, flown back to Istanbul, and removed from the tournament roster — a story that has dominated the pre-tournament news cycle and raised questions about who really controls access to this World Cup.

Who is Omar Artan?

  • Named CAF Men’s Referee of the Year 2025 — widely regarded as one of Africa’s top match officials.
  • FIFA-listed referee since 2018; experience at Africa Cup of Nations and continental club football.
  • Would have been Somalia’s first referee at a World Cup — a milestone for a nation rebuilding its football culture.

What happened at Miami?

According to Artan and officials quoted by international media:

  1. He arrived in Miami from Istanbul with a valid US visa and diplomatic passport arranged to ease travel after earlier visa difficulties.
  2. US Customs and Border Protection (CBP) held him for an extended inspection — Artan described roughly 11 hours of questioning.
  3. He was then detained further before being placed on a return flight to Istanbul.
  4. CBP said he was “inadmissible due to vetting concerns” without publishing a detailed public reason.

Somalia is among countries subject to US travel restrictions under the current administration. Exceptions exist, but Artan’s case shows that even FIFA-appointed officials with paperwork are not automatically exempt from intensive border screening.

FIFA’s response

FIFA confirmed Artan cannot train or officiate because all tournament referees are required to base themselves in the United States for logistics and security. The governing body said it is not involved in visa decisions and was told Artan’s status “will not be changed at present.”

Critically, officials in Mexico and Canada matches also depend on the US hub — Artan could not referee in North America without entering the US, even for games outside American stadiums.

Reactions

  • Somalia — government advisers called the decision harmful to football’s “fairness and merit”; fans in Mogadishu held images of Artan at domestic league games.
  • CAIR and civil-rights groups linked the case to broader travel-ban policy, arguing nationality-based exclusions clash with tournament ideals.
  • Media analysis — BBC and others asked whether FIFA has “lost control” of its own event when a member of its officiating pool cannot enter the host country.
  • US officials — later briefings referenced “association with suspected members of terror organizations” in anonymous comments; Artan and Somali officials dispute any wrongdoing.

Why fans care

This is not a distant federation story. It signals how immigration policy may affect players, staff, fans, and even FIFA’s own delegations across a 39-day tournament in three countries. If a referee of the year cannot cross the border, supporters wonder what awaits ordinary fans travelling from restricted countries — and whether the World Cup’s “global party” narrative holds up in practice.

For Somali fans, Artan’s hero’s welcome on return home turned personal disappointment into national pride — while the sport’s leaders face uncomfortable questions about politics overshadowing the pitch.
