Iran at World Cup 2026: tickets revoked, base camp in Tijuana, matchday-only US entry

Updated 11 June 2026 · TOP FAN VOTE editorial · Draft

Iran’s road to the World Cup has been defined by geopolitics as much as football. Days before the tournament, the country’s federation said its fan-ticket allocation was revoked, while the national team relocated its training base from Arizona to Tijuana, Mexico and negotiated visa conditions that allow US entry only on match days. For Iranian supporters — including a large community in Los Angeles — the uncertainty is personal and expensive.

Ticket allocation: what Iran says happened

Under FIFA rules, each participating federation typically receives about 8% of tickets per match to sell to its supporters. The Football Federation of the Islamic Republic of Iran (FFIRI) said it had already begun distributing tickets when the allocation was pulled back shortly before the tournament.

Iran linked the move to US regulatory constraints, including Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) rules that limit how US-based entities can process payments tied to Iran. If no compliant workaround is found, those seats may return to general sale — leaving fans who planned travel in limbo.

FIFA said it is “working closely” with the Iranian federation to find compliant ways for supporters to attend. As of publication, fans should treat any non-FIFA ticket channel as high risk.

Base camp: Tucson → Tijuana

In May 2026 Iran moved its World Cup training camp from Tucson, Arizona to Tijuana, Mexico, citing unwillingness by US authorities to host the squad under normal conditions amid regional conflict and diplomatic tension.

Practical consequences for the team:

  • Longer cross-border logistics for training and media.
  • US group matches still require entry to American stadiums (Los Angeles area for Iran’s Group G opener vs New Zealand).
  • Visa conditions reportedly require fly-in on match day and exit afterward rather than a continuous US stay.
  • Multiple backroom staff — FFIRI cited about 15 administrative officials — were denied US visas in early June.

Players and visas

After weeks of uncertainty during the US–Israel conflict with Iran, US visas for players were issued roughly ten days before Iran’s first match — but not for every member of the travelling party. Iran had previously presented FIFA with a list of participation conditions, including treatment of individuals with past military service linked to the IRGC — a politically sensitive topic in US entry law.

The squad has had limited competitive football since Iran’s domestic league paused in February, adding sporting — not just political — pressure before kickoff.

What it means for fans

  • Los Angeles has one of the largest Iranian diaspora communities in the US — a revoked ticket pool hits local families planning to attend at SoFi/Inglewood venues.
  • Travelling supporters need to monitor FIFA’s official ticket channels only; resale scams spike before high-demand games.
  • Border and visa policy remain unpredictable — build buffer days and avoid assuming last-minute entry fixes.

Broader pattern

Iran’s story sits alongside other entry disputes at this World Cup — Somali referee Omar Artan denied at Miami, Iraqi delegation members questioned for hours in Chicago, Swiss forward Breel Embolo delayed by visa flags. Together they paint a tournament where immigration policy is part of the matchday experience, not a background detail.
